Joseph Byerly born in Michigan in 1923. Grewing he was trying to get through the depression living in a house with grandparents that were direct immigrants from Bavaria. Growing up with both German and English in his household. Still in high school when Pearl Harbor was attacked the very athletic Byerly turned down a scholarship to play basketball at Notre Dame and joined the 101st Airborne Division were he got nicknamed Jumpin Joe. In training he specialized as a demolitions expert once he made it to England after training was complete he was selected for a few volunteer jumps. In May in April of 1944 where he covertly dropped into occupied France to deliver 250,000 dollars in gold to the French Resistance as funding from the Allies. After making it back safe both times his first large-scale combat jump was with 101st airborne during Operation Overlord.

Beyrle in Ramsbury, 1943

One of the biggest problems for the paratroopers in this operation though was that because of all the anti-aircraft fire the planes dropping them off were going too fast and sometimes to slow and when they jumped everybody got scattered all across their drop zones far from where they are supposed to be. Joe had to jump out of his plane exceptionally low at about 400 feet and landed on top of the church at Saint count Dumont and was able to slide down off the side to a cemetary and run off into the night under German fire. After this he was able to blow up a German power station and while walking towards his objective of two bridges that his unit was supposed to take. He crawled hedge rows into a German machine-gun nest and was promptly captured and thus begins one of the most amazing and incredible POWs stories of the war. After he was captured his tags were taken and given to a German soldier who in an American uniform was going to attempt to mingle with American troops but it failed resulting in American soldiers finding his tags and listing him as dead.

The US War Department telegram sent to Beyrle's family, incorrectly telling them of his death, September 1944

After this first capture he was marched with other POWs to a holding area in Karen tan where he received a minor wound from advancing Allied artillery. In the chaos though the prisoners got separated from their handlers and as the German patrol came back to collect the POWs he and a few soldiers made a run for it and got away wandering the Normandy countryside for a few hours looking for friendly troops until they were recaptured again. He was then sent to saint-lo where he survived another allied attack this time in bomber form before he was taken to a monastery and questioned heavily about his German heritage and why he was fighting with the Americans. About the incident he said. " I was interrogated 20 to 24 hours a day they were trying to get all the usual questions answered why me as a German was I fighting for the Jews Roosevelt and Morgenthau against my own people some time during the questioning I called a German officer an sob and woke up several days later in a hospital with a big headache and a bashed head and later I was taken back to the monastery". NAfter this he was put into a forced labour repairing railroad tracks around northern France having to dodge even more friendly bomber raids on a regular basis. After that he was taking eastward through France ending up at a warehouse outside of Paris and eventually in Stalag 11a in Lindbergh Germany where he was finally able to write home surely to the surprise of his family who had been told he was dead and was registered as a pow.

The German record with Beyrle's details as a prisoner of war

After this he was was marched eastward towards Stalag 3 C being the person that he was though Joe was not able to sit out the rest of the war and quickly tried to figure out ways to escape. His first escape attempt with two other POWs by the names of Breuer and Quinn was pulled off by bribing the German guards with cigarettes provided to them by the Red Cross and boarding a train headed for Poland where they would meet up with either the Red Army or the Polish resistance. Their hopes were dashed quickly though is when the train stopped they realized they had bordered one headed for Berlin. They were soon found by the Gestapo where they were tortured and interrogated for the next week it was decided that they were going to be shot as spies but before this happened the army reclaimed them as POWs once back in the hands of the German army. They were sent back to Stalag 3c however upon returning they heard on a contraband radio that they kept secret about how the Soviets had smashed through Army Group centre and we're getting fairly close to the camp where they were being kept really only a few days walking distance away so the trio attempted their escape again. This time sneaking out an empty food containers leaving the camp however on the way out the cart or whatever they were on tipped over to where they had to make a run for it with only by berly getting out alive heading towards the Russian lines after some walking he ran into a Russian tank unit where he identified himself as an American instead of asking to be taken to the rear and trying to head home after his long harrowing journey the mad lad asked if he could fight alongside them the first russian officer he asked promptly said no to his request but upon going to the battalion commander who was actually the rather famous Aleksandra Samusenko the only female tank commander of the 1st Guards tank army who became well known for her exploits at Kursk. His request was granted and he was able to fight alongside them.

Joe fought with them for about a month putting his demolitions expertise to good use and freed his previous POWs camp Stalag 3c in the process he was later wounded and taken into a Soviet field hospital. He found was way back to the US embassy there were still a few more issues for him though as when he got to the US embassy both the Americans and Soviets were suspicious of him because according to their records Joe Byerly was dead since his tags had been found in Normandy and he was kept under guard until his fingerprints were able to be used to verify his identity out of fear of him being a spy or something. Joe returned home in April of 1945 and was able to spend VE day with his family when he came home he found that because they figured he was dead there had been a full service for him in the town's Church. He would later get married by the very same priest who did his wake in the very same church that his funeral was in. Joe remained active in veterans organizations throughout his life and was recognized by both President Clinton and President Yeltsin in 1944 at the 50th anniversary of the D-day landings.
